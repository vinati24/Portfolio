01. About Me

I am an AI and machine learning engineer with a focused specialisation in biomedicine and healthcare.

My background spans computer science, health informatics, and an MSc in AI for Biomedicine and Healthcare at UCL, where I hold a Global Masters Scholarship. That foundation lets me work across the breadth of what this field demands, from deep learning architectures and NLP pipelines to wearable signal processing, affective computing, and clinical decision support, while staying grounded in the real-world constraints of healthcare: regulatory, ethical, and human.

I believe the most important question in health AI right now isn't "how accurate is the model?" It's "can the people who need to act on it actually understand it?"

That conviction runs through everything I build. The possibilities in this space are expanding fast: personalised medicine, passive early detection, and research at a scale that wasn't feasible a decade ago. But potential only matters if it translates into systems that are safe, interpretable, and deployable in the real world. Explainability and responsible AI aren't principles I layer on at the end; they're where I start.

What draws me to this field is the combination of intellectual rigour and genuine consequence. These are hard problems, and getting them right actually matters.

I'm looking for opportunities across AI for biomedicine and healthcare, whether in research, engineering, or product, where the goal is to build something that doesn't just work technically, but makes a measurable difference to how care is delivered, understood, or advanced. If that's the kind of work you're doing, I'd love to connect.

03. Featured Projects

⚖️
UCL Statistical NLP

Legal Hallucination Evaluation Pipeline

Every traditional metric - ROUGE, BERTScore, DeBERTa-NLI - scored near random chance (~0.5 AUROC) at detecting hallucinations in UK Supreme Court summaries, even with retrieval. So we built an LLM-as-a-Judge pipeline instead. GPT-5.4-Mini hit 0.78 F1, Gemini matched at 0.77, while Llama-3.1-8B lagged at 0.58. The hardest category? Faithfulness - subtle hedging removals dropped all models below 0.5 F1. Biggest learning: factual errors are easy to catch, but linguistic overreach is where LLMs still struggle.

UCL Affective Computing

Tetris Emotion Recognition

Classifying boredom, excitement, and frustration from physiological signals during Tetris gameplay. Macro-average F1 of ~31% for frustration and ~30% for excitement - and that was the point. High-intensity states had 0% recall - the physiological feature space simply couldn't separate adjacent intensity levels with 7 participants. Authored a 2,800-word individual critique grounding the work in the EU AI Act and Russell's Circumplex Model. Key takeaway: sometimes the most honest result is showing where the method breaks.

🏥

Clinical Risk Alert Dashboard

The hardest problem wasn't building the model - it was stopping a 1B-parameter LLM from hallucinating medical crises. When a patient had 9,872 steps but excellent 95ms HRV, the raw Llama-3.2-1B would panic and fabricate a cardiovascular emergency. I solved it with a Deterministic-Generative Hybrid Architecture - decoupling clinical logic from the LLM entirely, so it only receives isolated, pre-validated instructions. Result: 100% logical consistency across all safety audit cases, with CTGAN synthetic data verified via Jensen-Shannon divergence for HIPAA compliance.

❤️

Trustworthy Heart Failure Prediction

Stacked ensemble achieving 0.936 AUC-ROC and 0.890 F1 with CatBoost as the primary driver (coefficient 2.506). But the real story was in what broke: DiCE counterfactuals suggested raising a 60-year-old's MaxHR to 188 bpm - physically impossible and clinically dangerous. The model found mathematical holes in decision boundaries, not medical ones. This taught me that clinical AI without biological constraints isn't trustworthy AI, no matter how good the metrics look.
